The Ethical Hiring Manifesto

We believe that hiring is a human activity, and that every person who applies for a job deserves to be treated with dignity, respect, and honesty. The way organisations hire people says everything about who they really are. We wrote this manifesto because we believe the hiring process is broken, and that fixing it requires not just good intentions but binding commitments.

These are the principles we stand by.

1. Every candidate is a person, not a data point

Behind every application is a human being who took the time to express interest in working with you. That act alone deserves acknowledgement and respect. We reject any process that reduces people to keywords, scores, or pattern matches. We commit to seeing candidates as whole people, with histories, hopes, and potential that no algorithm can measure.

Too many organisations treat applications as inventory to be processed. Applicant tracking systems silently discard CVs because a keyword is missing. Recruiters bulk-download profiles from LinkedIn and send identical messages to hundreds of people, never reading a single one. Candidates spend hours tailoring applications only to receive an instant automated rejection, or worse, nothing at all. Some companies repost the same role month after month, collecting applications with no intention of filling the position, using the pipeline as a comfort blanket or a market research exercise. Every one of these practices treats people as raw material. We refuse to participate in this, and we refuse to accept it as normal.

2. Communication is not optional

Silence is the most common and most damaging failure in hiring. Every candidate who engages with your process has the right to know where they stand. We commit to timely, honest, and personal communication at every stage. No ghosting. No automated dead ends. No silence disguised as process.

The reality today is shameful. Candidates attend interviews, invest hours in preparation, take time off work, arrange childcare, travel across the country, and then hear nothing. Weeks pass. Emails go unanswered. Recruiters who were enthusiastic and responsive before the interview become unreachable afterwards. Some companies use phrases like "we'll keep your CV on file" as a polite way of saying goodbye without the honesty of actually saying it. Others string candidates along for weeks with vague promises of "next steps" while quietly hiring someone else. Recruitment agencies are particularly guilty of disappearing once a candidate is no longer useful to them, treating people as a means to a commission rather than as individuals who deserve closure. This behaviour causes real harm. It creates anxiety, erodes confidence, and teaches people that the professional world does not care about them. We refuse to be part of that lesson.

3. Feedback is a duty, not a favour, and it must be regulated

When a candidate is not selected, they deserve to know why. "We decided to go with another candidate" is not feedback. It is a dismissal.

We believe that feedback in hiring should follow a structured, transparent process with clear obligations at each stage. At a minimum, this means: when a candidate is screened out, they must be told what criteria were used and why they did not meet them. When a candidate is rejected after an interview, they must receive specific, written feedback explaining what was assessed, how they performed, and what led to the decision. When a final hiring decision is made, the successful criteria must be documented and available to all candidates who reached that stage on request.

This is not about bureaucracy. It is about accountability. Vague rejections protect the employer, not the candidate. Structured feedback forces honest decision-making, reduces bias, and gives people something they can actually use.

We believe this process should be enforceable by law. An independent body, similar to an Ombudsman for hiring practices, should have the authority to investigate complaints from candidates who were denied feedback or given feedback that does not meet the required standard. This body should have the power to issue fines and, in cases of repeated or systemic failure, to publicly report organisations that consistently fail to treat candidates with the transparency they deserve.

Feedback is not a courtesy. It is a right.

4. Human contact must be at the heart of hiring

We believe that no candidate should pass through a hiring process without meaningful human interaction. Automated screening tools, AI-driven filtering, and algorithmic shortlisting remove the very thing that makes hiring work: human judgement, intuition, and connection. Technology should support hiring, never replace the people in it. We commit to ensuring that every candidate interacts with a real person who can listen, respond, and make decisions with empathy.

The trend is moving in the wrong direction. Companies now use AI to screen CVs, score candidates, analyse video interviews for facial expressions and tone of voice, and even generate rejection emails that mimic human warmth while being entirely mechanical. Some organisations have built hiring funnels where a candidate can be rejected at multiple stages without ever speaking to a human being. Chatbots ask screening questions. Algorithms rank answers. Automated emails deliver the verdict. The entire experience is designed for the efficiency of the employer, not the dignity of the candidate. Recruiters increasingly rely on Boolean searches and automated outreach, contacting dozens of people simultaneously with messages that pretend to be personal but clearly are not. When a candidate replies, they often discover the recruiter has not even read their profile. We believe this is not just poor practice. It is dehumanising, and it must stop. If your hiring process can run without a single human being involved, it is not a hiring process. It is a sorting machine.

5. Age is not a measure of ability

Ageism in hiring is real, pervasive, and deeply harmful. We reject the assumption that younger means more adaptable, or that older means less relevant. Experience has value. Fresh perspective has value. Neither belongs to a single generation. We commit to evaluating candidates on their skills, their potential, and their ability to contribute, regardless of their age, and we actively oppose any screening practice that uses age as a proxy for capability.

Ageism in recruitment is often disguised behind language that sounds neutral but is anything but. Job adverts that ask for a "digital native" or someone from a "fast-paced, young team" are signalling that older candidates are not welcome. Recruiters filter candidates by graduation year, which is simply age discrimination with extra steps. Companies set maximum years of experience in their requirements, not because they cannot afford seniority but because they have decided that anyone over a certain age does not fit their culture. Culture fit itself has become a euphemism for hiring people who look, think, and age the same way. Some organisations will interview an older candidate to meet diversity quotas but never seriously consider hiring them. Others assume that someone with thirty years of experience will be too expensive, too set in their ways, or too difficult to manage, without ever asking a single question to find out. Meanwhile, the same organisations happily hire inexperienced candidates and spend months training them, calling it an investment. This double standard is both irrational and unjust, and it wastes talent on an enormous scale.

6. Empathy is a professional skill, not a soft one

Hiring decisions affect people's lives, their financial security, their confidence, and their sense of worth. We commit to approaching every interaction in the hiring process with genuine empathy. This means understanding that candidates may be anxious, vulnerable, or in difficult circumstances. It means never using power imbalances to pressure, rush, or dismiss. It means remembering what it felt like to be on the other side.

The absence of empathy in hiring is everywhere, and it often comes from people who would consider themselves professional and fair. Interviewers who check their phones during a conversation. Hiring managers who cancel interviews at the last minute, or simply do not show up, with no apology and no explanation. Panels that ask deliberately stressful questions not to assess resilience but to enjoy the power of watching someone struggle. Recruiters who call candidates to tell them they were unsuccessful and then rush through the conversation because they have ten more calls to make. Companies that reject a candidate and then contact them weeks later when their first choice falls through, expecting gratitude rather than recognising the insult. Organisations that require candidates to disclose salary expectations early in the process, then use that information as leverage rather than engaging in honest negotiation. Every one of these behaviours reveals a hiring culture that has forgotten, or never learned, that the person on the other side of the table is a human being with feelings, not an item on a to-do list.

7. Job descriptions must be honest

We commit to writing job descriptions that accurately reflect the role, the expectations, and the compensation. No inflated titles to disguise junior roles. No "competitive salary" when the budget is already fixed. No impossible wish lists designed to justify underpaying the person you eventually hire. Honesty in a job advert is the first act of respect towards a future colleague.

Dishonest job descriptions are so common that candidates have learned to treat them as fiction. A role advertised as "flexible working" turns out to mean one day from home per month, with manager approval. A "senior" title comes with a junior salary because the company uses titles instead of pay to attract talent. Requirements list fifteen technologies, three certifications, and five years of experience in a framework that has existed for two, ensuring that no candidate feels fully qualified, which shifts negotiating power to the employer. Some adverts describe a role that does not exist yet, or one that will change completely within weeks of the hire. Others are posted with an internal candidate already selected, turning the entire process into theatre to satisfy a procurement policy. Recruitment agencies regularly advertise roles at inflated salaries to attract applicants, only to reveal the real figure later once the candidate is emotionally invested. Some adverts omit the salary entirely, forcing candidates to go through multiple rounds before discovering the role pays far less than they need. All of this wastes people's time and trust, and it poisons the relationship between employer and candidate before it has even begun.

8. The process must respect people's time

Hiring processes that stretch across months, involve five or more interview rounds, or require unpaid work samples treat candidates as though their time has no value. We commit to hiring processes that are as short and focused as reasonably possible, that communicate timelines upfront, and that never ask candidates to invest time disproportionate to the stage they are at.

The disrespect for candidates' time has become normalised to the point where people accept absurd demands as the cost of getting a job. Companies routinely ask candidates to complete multi-hour take-home assignments before they have even spoken to a human being. Some require full presentations, business plans, or design projects that represent days of unpaid work, with no guarantee that the output will not be used by the company regardless of whether the candidate is hired. Interview processes that involve six, seven, or eight rounds spread across two months are not thorough. They are indecisive, and they punish the candidate for the organisation's inability to make decisions. Recruiters schedule calls and then reschedule them repeatedly, treating the candidate's calendar as infinitely flexible while protecting their own. Companies ask candidates to be "available at short notice" for interviews, ignoring the fact that the candidate has a current job, a family, and commitments that matter. Some processes require candidates to travel long distances for in-person interviews that could easily have been conducted remotely, offering no reimbursement and no acknowledgement that the candidate has just spent half a day and their own money for a thirty-minute conversation. Time is the one resource candidates cannot recover, and every unnecessary demand on it is a statement that the employer's convenience matters more than the candidate's life.

9. Bias must be actively challenged, not passively acknowledged

Unconscious bias training is not enough. We commit to building processes that structurally reduce the opportunity for bias to influence decisions. This means standardised evaluation criteria, diverse interview panels, and regular audits of hiring outcomes. It means measuring who applies, who progresses, and who gets hired, and being honest about the patterns we find.

Most organisations claim to value diversity, but their hiring processes tell a different story. Companies that say they are equal opportunity employers still screen out candidates with foreign-sounding names, gaps in their CVs, or degrees from universities they do not recognise. Recruiters make snap judgements based on a candidate's accent, appearance, or the neighbourhood they live in. Interview panels made up entirely of one demographic consistently hire people who look and sound like themselves, and call it merit. Some organisations use "culture fit" as a filter that in practice means "people we would want to have a drink with," which systematically excludes anyone who is different. Others ask about hobbies, family plans, or social interests in interviews, gathering information that has nothing to do with the job but everything to do with bias. Pregnancy and parental status are still used, quietly and illegally, to filter out candidates. Disability is still treated as a risk rather than a reality to be accommodated. Many companies celebrate diversity in their marketing while their hiring data tells a completely different story, one they never publish because they know what it would reveal. Acknowledging that bias exists is not the same as doing something about it. We commit to doing something about it.

10. Hiring ethics should be a matter of law, not goodwill

We believe that ethical hiring should not depend on the conscience of individual employers. The principles in this manifesto should be reflected in employment law and actively enforced. Candidates should have legal protections against ghosting after interviews, age discrimination in screening, and fully automated decision-making that removes human accountability.

Specifically, we call for the establishment of an independent Hiring Practices Ombudsman with statutory powers to receive and investigate complaints from candidates, to audit employer hiring processes, to impose meaningful fines on organisations that fail to meet their obligations, and to publish regular reports on hiring standards across industries. This body should operate with the same authority and independence as existing employment regulators, and access to it should be free for all candidates.

Regulation is not the enemy of good hiring. It is what makes good hiring the norm rather than the exception. We call on governments to recognise that the hiring process is part of the employment relationship and to regulate it accordingly, with the same seriousness applied to workplace rights.

We sign this manifesto because we believe that how you hire people is how you treat people. And how you treat people is who you are.

If you agree with these principles, share them. If you hire people, live by them. If you make policy, enshrine them.
